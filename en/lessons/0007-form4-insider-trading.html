Form 3/4/5 & Insider Trading
Executives voting with their feet -- how to read their signals

Lesson 0006 covered what external institutions (Vanguard, BlackRock, etc.) are buying and selling. This lesson covers what company insiders (CEO, CFO, directors) are buying and selling themselves.

Insider transaction data has the highest signal-to-noise ratio in the SEC disclosure system -- because insiders know the company far better than any external investor. Their buying or selling is typically more informative than institutional holdings changes. But only if you read it correctly; otherwise, you can be seriously misled.

Insider trading is not an "event" -- it is a continuous data stream. A single transaction means nothing, but a weighted aggregate sentiment (InsiderSentiment metric) can tell you "what this company's executives are thinking about the future."

What is Section 16 and who does it cover

Section 16 is Article 16 of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, specifically governing corporate insiders' holdings and transaction disclosures. Three key categories:

CategoryMeaningExamples
OfficerCompany officers, typically VP and aboveCEO, CFO, COO, SVP/EVP
DirectorBoard membersIndependent directors, parent company representatives
10% OwnerShareholder holding >10% of the companyFounders, parent companies, large passive shareholders

⚠️ Blind spot for Chinese ADR companies

Section 16 only applies to insiders holding U.S. registered securities. Executives of Chinese ADR companies (UXIN, ATHM) typically hold Cayman/VIE-level interests -- these are not subject to SEC Section 16.

Result: UXIN/ATHM have virtually no Form 4 data. This does not mean "no insider trading"; it means "insider trading is not within SEC disclosure scope." Our system marks has_section16 = False in config.py, and the Dashboard displays "Not Applicable" rather than "No Data."

Difference between the three insider forms

Insider transaction disclosure uses three forms, each with different submission cadence and content:

FormFull NameWhen FiledContent
Form 3Initial StatementWithin 10 days of becoming an insiderInitial holdings statement (one-time)
Form 4Statement of ChangesWithin 2 business days of transactionEvery purchase/sale must be reported
Form 5Annual StatementWithin 45 days after fiscal year endAnnual summary (exempt transactions)

Form 4 is the most important -- it has the best timeliness (2 business days) and covers all transactions. Forms 3 and 5 are primarily "catch-up" filings.

Form 4 structure: Table I vs Table II

Form 4 consists of two tables. The distinction is critical: 

Table I — Non-Derivative Securities (direct holdings trades)
  Fields:
    - Title of Security
    - Transaction Date
    - Transaction Code           P=Purchase, S=Sale, G=Gift, A=Award, F=Tax withholding
    - Amount                     Number of shares
    - Price                      Per-share price
    - Amount After               Holdings after transaction

Table II — Derivative Securities (options/derivatives changes)
  Similar fields, but units are "option contracts" not "shares"
  Common Codes: M=Exercise, A=Award (options), C=Conversion

Common Transaction Codes

CodeMeaningSignal?
PPurchase (open market)✅✅✅ Strong positive -- insider spending their own money
SSale (open market)✅ Weak negative -- could be diversification, not necessarily bearish
FTax withholding❌ Almost no signal -- standard tax withholding on option exercise
GGift❌ Weak signal -- common for charity/family trust
AAward/Exercise❌ No signal -- part of compensation, not active trading
MOption ExerciseDepends on post-exercise selling (Code F/S pairing)

Most common misinterpretation

Misreading "large Code F tax withholding sales" as "insider is bearish." This is the biggest pitfall. When executives exercise options in bulk under a 10b5-1 plan, it generates large Code F (tax withholding) and Code S (sale) transactions simultaneously.

Correct approach: Look at Code P (open market purchases) amounts, and net selling after stripping out option-exercise-related sales. Summing all sale codes directly will drown the signal in noise.

Correct algorithm for Insider Sentiment

Simply counting the number of buy vs sell transactions is meaningless (10b5-1 plans generate a high volume of paired trades). The correct method is weighted scoring: 

InsiderSentiment = Σ(Purchase_Amount_i × Role_Weight_i × Transaction_Code_Weight_i)
                  - Σ(Sale_Amount_j × Role_Weight_j × Transaction_Code_Weight_j)

Role Weights:
  CEO / CFO                   2.0    Top decision makers
  10% Owner                   1.5    Large shareholders
  Director                    1.0    Board of directors
  EVP / SVP / VP              0.8    Executive layer

Transaction Code Weights:
  P (Open market purchase)    1.0
  S (Open market sale)        1.0
  F (Tax withholding)         0.2
  G (Gift)                    0.3
  A (Option award)            0.0
  M (Option exercise)         0.0   (look at post-exercise sales only)

Normalized to [-100, +100]:
  > +30  → Bullish signal
  -30 ~ +30 → Neutral
  < -30  → Bearish signal

Note that F weight is 0.2, not 0 -- we retain a faint signal (large tax-withholding sales may reflect massive exercise = concentrated cash-out), but it does not dominate the conclusion.

Avoid this common mistake: One month before the 10-K release, a large number of insider sales appear (preset by a 10b5-1 plan). This does not mean "insiders lack confidence in the annual report" -- it may be scheduled diversification under a 10b5-1 framework. You should check the 10b5-1 plan filing date (usually in 8-K Item 8.01 or DEF 14A), not just the trades themselves.

Form 144 -- the "trailer" for selling

Form 144 is a different type of insider signal -- it is an advance notice, not an after-the-fact disclosure.

Key Facts about Form 144
Trigger: Insider plans to sell ≥5,000 shares or ≥$50,000
Timing: 1-3 business days before the planned sale
Validity: Sale must be completed within 90 days of Form 144 filing
Aggregate market value: Estimated value based on filing-date stock price (not actual sale price)

Key interpretation:

  • Form 144 does not mean "already sold" -- it only means "preparing to sell." The plan may not be executed
  • But consecutive Form 144 filings = sustained selling pressure signal, be cautious
  • When using Form 144 to predict selling pressure, check the "actual execution rate" (OpenInsider has this data)

Special handling for Chinese competitors (operational rules)

Among our 5 competitors, Uxin (UXIN) and Autohome (ATHM) are Chinese ADRs. They have no Form 4 / Form 144 data, but they have parent-level HK/A-share market disclosures.

System processing rules

1. config.py flag: has_section16 = False
2. Dashboard display:
    - Shows "Not Applicable" rather than "No Data" (to avoid being mistaken for a collection failure)
    - Annotation: "Insiders hold VIE-level interests, not subject to SEC disclosure"
3. Anomaly alerts:
    - If a Chinese ADR suddenly has Form 4 data → red alert
    - This is a rare event, indicating insiders deliberately moved interests from Cayman to U.S. level for trading
    - Historically very rare, but when it happens, it almost always signals something significant

Quiz

Q1: An executive exercises options in bulk under a 10b5-1 plan, generating 100 Code F (tax withholding) trades. How should your system interpret this?

Click to see answer

It should be interpreted as virtually no negative signal -- this is standard operating procedure under a 10b5-1 plan. Code F has a weight of 0.2, so 100 trades × 0.2 = 20 units of noise contribution. If you only counted trades, you would incorrectly conclude "massive insider selling." With the weighted algorithm, the conclusion would be neutral or slightly negative. The judgment must also account for when the 10b5-1 plan was filed (usually in 8-K Item 8.01 or DEF 14A). If the filing date was 3-6 months ago, these are almost certainly pre-scheduled trades.

Q2: The Dashboard for Uxin (UXIN) shows "Insider Trading: Not Applicable." How do you distinguish this from a data collection failure?

Click to see answer

These two must be clearly distinguished because their meanings are completely different. "Not Applicable" = the company's structure means Section 16 disclosure does not apply (Chinese ADR insiders hold VIE-level interests), with the system marking has_section16 = False in config.py. "No Data" = collection failed or no Form 4 was filed in the period (rare, since Form 4 is mandatory disclosure within 2 business days). The Dashboard UI should use different colors and text: "Not Applicable" in gray with an explanatory annotation; "No Data" in red, potentially indicating an API failure.

Q3: You see Carvana's CFO sold $5M in stock under a 10b5-1 plan, but at the same time a VP bought $1M on the open market. How does your system score this?

Click to see answer

It depends on the specific Code of that $5M sale. If it is Code F (tax withholding) + associated Code S (10b5-1 plan sale), then the weight is 0.2 + 1.0 × role weight 2.0 = 2.2 units of negative contribution. But the VP's open market purchase (Code P) = 1.0 × 0.8 = 0.8 units of positive contribution. If the amounts are in a 5:1 ratio, the result is net negative ~-3,400 units, but after normalization it may land in the -30 to 0 range, with a conclusion of "cautious", not "bearish." So even though the dollar amount is large, because it is a programmatic sale under a 10b5-1 plan, the conclusion should be discounted.
