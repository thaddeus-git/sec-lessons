Schedule 13D/G & Activist Investors
Who is trying to change the company, and how to identify them

The previous two lessons covered 13F (institutional holdings) and Form 4 (insider trading). This lesson covers a rarer but even higher-signal class of disclosures: Schedule 13D and 13G -- mandatory filings for shareholders owning more than 5% of a company.

13D/G filings occur only a few hundred times per year (versus 13F's 5,000-6,000 filers × 4 quarters = 20,000+ quarterly reports), but each one represents "someone has built a large position in your company." This is the highest signal-to-noise ratio data in institutional holdings.

The P4 "Activists" section of the IHS Markit report is built almost entirely on 13D/G data. An activist appearing on the P4 list = a critically important event.

13D vs 13G: Core differences

The key difference between these two forms lies in the filer's intent:

DimensionSchedule 13DSchedule 13G
TriggerOwnership >5% and intent to influence company operationsOwnership >5% and no intent to influence (pure investment)
Filing deadlineWithin 10 days of reaching 5%Annual filing (typically before Feb 14)
Who filesActivist investors, hedge funds, activist fundsIndex funds, pension funds, passive institutions
Subsequent updatesEach buy/sell >1% requires amendmentAnnual updates only
Disclosure contentHoldings + detailed purpose statement ("we intend to push for a spin-off," etc.)Holdings + brief statement
Competitor monitoring value⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very high⭐ Low (passive holdings)

Key judgment point: 13D Item 4

13D's Item 4 (Purpose of Transaction) is the most valuable field. Here, the activist states "why we bought this company" -- which might be "demanding a spin-off of the e-commerce business," "opposing a proposed acquisition," or "nominating new director candidates."

This text is typically strongly worded ("we believe the company is significantly undervalued...") because it triggers a sharp stock price reaction, and the activist themselves wants the market to see the signal.

How to determine whether an institution is an Activist

IHS Markit maintains an activist fund database that we cannot fully replicate. However, we can use the following rules for approximate judgment:

Professional criteria (IHS standard)

1. Historical 13D filing records
   → Institutions that repeatedly file 13D = professional activists
2. Proxy fight / shareholder proposal history
   → Have they nominated their own candidates at shareholder meetings?
3. Public activist campaigns
   → Open letters demanding CEO change, business spin-off, share buyback?
4. Fund characteristics
   → Hedge fund background + concentrated holdings + high turnover

Our approximate method

1. Maintain a static list (config.py)
   - Known activist funds (sourced from WhaleWisdom 13D tracking, public news)
   - Our system has an ACTIVIST_INSTITUTIONS list (currently 8)

2. Populate the activism_level field
   - "often"    Multiple campaigns (typical activist)
   - "occasional"  Participates occasionally (mostly passive, occasional activist behavior)
   - "passive"  Purely passive holdings

3. Periodically supplement the list
   - Search WhaleWisdom / SEC EDGAR for 13D filings
   - Incremental update of ACTIVIST_INSTITUTIONS monthly

Common Activist strategy types

Strategy 1: Board seats

Activist nominates its own director candidates, seeking board representation. Attempting to influence corporate governance from within.

Signal strength: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ -- highest level, open confrontation with management.

Strategy 2: Capital allocation

Demanding dividends, share buybacks, business spin-offs, divestiture of non-core assets.

Signal strength: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ -- a vote of no confidence in existing management.

Strategy 3: M&A push

Requiring the company to sell itself, acquire specific targets, or oppose an existing merger plan.

Signal strength: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ -- may trigger a change of control.

Strategy 4: Operational improvement

Demanding cost cuts, CEO change, strategy shift, or shutdown of underperforming units.

Signal strength: ⭐⭐⭐ -- believes the company has hidden value, but not yet at confrontation stage.

13D "Item 4" -- a real example

Below is a simplified version of Item 4 from a 2024 13D filing by an activist (adapted from a public case):

Item 4. Purpose of Transaction.

The Reporting Person acquired the Subject Common Shares because
it believes the Subject Common Shares are significantly undervalued
and represent an attractive investment opportunity.

The Reporting Person intends to engage in discussions with the
Issuer's board of directors and management regarding a broad range
of strategic alternatives, including but not limited to:
  (i) the separation of the Company's underperforming business unit;
  (ii) a meaningful reduction in operating expenses;
  (iii) a comprehensive review of capital allocation policy;
  (iv) potential board refreshment.

The Reporting Person may also seek to nominate one or more
directors at the next annual meeting of stockholders.

In plain English: "We believe this company is significantly undervalued. We will talk to the board -- demanding a spin-off, cost cuts, capital allocation changes, and board refreshment. If talks fail, we will nominate our own directors."

When this text is made public, the stock price typically rises 10-30% within 24-48 hours (the market expects the activist to drive value realization). However, medium-to-long-term performance depends on how much change the activist can actually push through.

The power of 13D lies in its "irreversibility." Once an activist publicly files a 13D, they enter a "public game" with the board -- they must push for change (or exit at a loss), and the company must respond. This is not the "passive holding" of a 13F; it is open warfare.

Design principles for this page:

  • Standalone page -- activist holdings are "warning-level" signals and should not be mixed with other institutions
  • Must annotate activism_level -- the signal strength difference between "often" and "occasional" is substantial
  • Show empty positions too -- $0 does not mean meaningless; it could mean the activist actively closed their position in a competitor (13F doesn't show this, but activist 13D/G must be disclosed)

Quiz

Q1: You see a hedge fund has filed a 13D, but Item 4 only says "we believe the company is undervalued" with no specific action items. What level of signal is this?

Click to see answer

Medium signal -- this is a "plant the flag, then watch and wait" activist. Filing a 13D is itself a public declaration (irreversible), but vague Item 4 wording suggests the activist hasn't decided what to push for yet. Possible explanations: (1) waiting for market reaction (take a small profit and exit); (2) negotiating with the company, taking a position first then applying pressure; (3) genuinely unhappy but without a plan to actually fight. Check the next 60-90 days for: a) supplementary 13D filings (updating Item 4 with concrete actions); b) initiation of a proxy fight; c) public director nominations. If none appear → may be a "paper tiger."

Q2: Can Uxin (UXIN) have 13D/G filings? Compared to 13F, how reliable is activist data for Chinese ADRs?

Click to see answer

UXIN is an ADR registered with the SEC, so 13D/G applies. However in practice: (1) UXIN has poor liquidity (low ADR trading volume), making it difficult for large institutions to build >5% positions (buying 5% would basically absorb all available supply); (2) activist involvement in China-concept stocks is far lower than in U.S. domestic companies (geographic, cultural, and legal environment differences); (3) even with a 13D filing, an activist's actual ability to drive change is limited (the company's main business is in China, the board is Chinese). So UXIN 13D/G appearances are low probability; when they do occur, the signal is actually stronger (rare event).

Q3: Our ACTIVIST_INSTITUTIONS static list only has 8 firms, while IHS Markit covers 12 (in its P4 example report). What would we miss from these missing 4?

Click to see answer

We would miss the first entry of a "non-mainstream" activist into a competitor. IHS's 12 firms have broader coverage (including activists that only operate in specific sectors), and the ones we are missing might be precisely the ones that first build a position in CVNA/KMX/AN -- a critical signal. But the marginal cost of filling this gap is high (either manual 13D monthly scanning or paid WhaleWisdom). MVP judgment: use the 8-firm list + manual monthly 13D scan + a "new activist detected" alert. This combination covers ~80% of signals at the best cost-benefit ratio.
